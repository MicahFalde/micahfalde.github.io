Plan Overview

This is a comprehensive homeschooling plan designed for a family using Classical Education with Jeffersonian principles integrated throughout. The plan covers all subjects from early childhood through high school graduation, with emphasis on deep proficiency in math and science, strong literacy, and classical learning methods.

Key Features:

  • Classical Trivium stages (Grammar, Logic, Rhetoric) drive the curriculum
  • Math and science receive daily deep-dive time with hands-on experiments
  • Strong literacy foundation through reading classics, copywork, and word roots
  • Bible reading and writing integrated throughout
  • Gifted math pathway for advanced learners
  • Adult learning adaptations for parents

This plan is designed to be flexible yet structured. You can adjust the pace based on your child's needs, but the overall progression follows classical education principles that have been refined over centuries.

Educational Principles

Classical Education

Based on the medieval Trivium (grammar, logic, rhetoric), adapted to child development stages by Dorothy Sayers in her 1947 essay "The Lost Tools of Learning."

  • Grammar Stage (ages 5–11): Absorb facts, rules, patterns
  • Logic Stage (ages 11–14): Question, analyze, reason
  • Rhetoric Stage (ages 14–18): Synthesize, persuade, create

Jeffersonian Principles

Based on Thomas Jefferson's 1779 "Bill for the More General Diffusion of Knowledge" — emphasis on geometry, navigation, practical sciences, and the critical window for language acquisition.

  • Primary school (ages 6–8): Reading/writing/arithmetic/history
  • Intermediate (ages 9–16): Languages/geometry/navigation
  • University (ages 17–19): Specialized sciences

Gifted Math Pathway

For children who excel in math, this pathway provides acceleration, depth, and enrichment through:

  • Early start (age 5)
  • Accelerated pace (skip grades if appropriate)
  • Depth (proofs, abstract thinking)
  • Enrichment (competition math, research)

The Trivium: Three Pillars of Classical Learning

The Trivium (Latin for "the three ways") is the foundation of classical education. Each stage corresponds to a child's natural learning tendencies and builds progressively:

Grammar Stage

Ages 5–11 (K–6)

The "what" stage — children naturally absorb facts, rules, vocabulary, and patterns through repetition, songs, memorization, and hands-on exploration.

Focus: Memorization, repetition, concrete examples

Logic Stage

Ages 11–14 (7–9)

The "why" stage — children begin to question, analyze, and reason. They want to understand how things work and why rules exist.

Focus: Analysis, argumentation, formal logic

Rhetoric Stage

Ages 14–18 (10–12)

The "how" stage — teenagers develop the ability to synthesize, persuade, create, and express their own voice and ideas clearly.

Focus: Synthesis, persuasion, original work

Jeffersonian Connection: Jefferson believed the ages 8–16 represent the "prime window" for memory and language acquisition. The Grammar and Logic stages align perfectly with this window — this is when Latin, math, and facts should be memorized heavily.

Daily Literacy Habits

Reading Aloud (15–20 min)

Read together daily — anything from fiction to nonfiction to newspapers. Models fluency and builds comprehension.

Copywork (10 min)

Copy passages from good writing daily. Builds grammar intuition, attention to detail, and a "mental library" of good sentences.

Word Roots (10 min)

Study one Greek/Latin root per day. Expands vocabulary and decoding ability. Makes reading more interesting.

Bible Reading (15 min)

Read Scripture daily. Copywork from Scripture improves writing and exposes to beautiful prose. Journal reflections.
