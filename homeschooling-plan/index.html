1. Plan Overview

This homeschooling plan is designed for a child who is not yet in school. It combines the Classical Education model — a time-tested approach focused on mastery of foundational skills before higher-order thinking — with select principles from Thomas Jefferson's educational philosophy, which emphasizes geometry, practical sciences, navigation, and the critical age window for language acquisition.

The plan covers all core subjects plus additional optional subjects, progressing from early childhood through high school graduation. Math and science receive emphasis with daily deep-dive time, while literacy (reading and writing) is woven into every stage through classical reading and writing methods.

Key Design Principles

  • Classical Trivium Stages: Grammar (ages 5–11), Logic (ages 11–14), Rhetoric (ages 14–18) — each stage matches natural cognitive development
  • Jeffersonian Emphasis: Geometry, navigation, and practical sciences integrated throughout; Latin introduced early to capitalize on the memory peak ages 8–16
  • Deep Proficiency in Math & Science: Daily dedicated time, hands-on experiments, real-world problem solving, not just textbook work
  • Strong Literacy: Reading aloud, copywork, grammar study, essay writing, debate, and oratory skills developed progressively
  • All Subjects Covered: Math, Language Arts, Science, History/Geography, Latin/Languages, Arts, Physical Education, plus optional Computer Science, Music, Foreign Language

2. Educational Models Compared

Before selecting a primary model, here's a brief overview of four major educational philosophies — including the one chosen (Classical), the one you mentioned (Jefferson), and two alternatives you can adapt:

Classical Education (Chosen Primary Model)

Pioneered by Dorothy Sayers in her 1947 essay "The Lost Tools of Learning," this model adapts the medieval Trivium (grammar, logic, rhetoric) to child development stages.

Pros

  • Strong curriculum backbone with clear progression
  • Matches natural cognitive development stages
  • Large community and resource base
  • Emphasizes critical thinking and communication
  • Works well for non-teachers (structured lessons)

Cons

  • Can feel rigid without adaptation
  • Heavy reliance on reading/writing
  • May not suit highly kinesthetic learners
  • Requires commitment to long-term structure

Jeffersonian Education (Integrated as STEM Supplement)

Based on Thomas Jefferson's 1779 "Bill for the More General Diffusion of Knowledge" — his plan for universal public education in Virginia.

Pros

  • Emphasizes practical, useful knowledge
  • Geometry and navigation build spatial reasoning
  • Languages prioritized during memory peak (ages 8–16)
  • Prepares for civic participation and self-reliance
  • Hands-on, nature-based learning in early years

Cons

  • Limited modern curriculum resources
  • Not a complete stand-alone model
  • Original plan focused on elite preparation
  • Requires supplementation for modern needs

Jefferson's Three-Stage System: Primary school (ages 6–8) for reading/writing/arithmetic/history; Intermediate (ages 9–16) for languages/geometry/navigation; University (ages 17–19) for specialized sciences.

Key Quote: "The most precious gift from Nature is the power of memory... The mind acquires new faculties at certain ages, and loses them at others."

Education of the American Founders (Historical Context)

The Founding Fathers were products of the same classical education that informs our plan. Understanding what they studied reveals why we teach what we teach.

The Colonial Education System: In the 1700s, education in America followed the English model: Latin grammar schools for boys (ages 7–14), followed by college (Harvard, Yale, Princeton). Education was fundamentally classical, Christian, and civic—designed to produce virtuous citizens and learned leaders.

The Founders' Own Words

John Adams (2nd President): "The Constitution, and the laws, have made no distinction between the sexes. They consider us as moral beings, and are alike attentive to our education, our morals, and our manners."

Thomas Jefferson (3rd President): "If a nation expects to be ignorant and free, in a state of civilization, it expects what never was and never will be."

George Washington (1st President): "Promote as much as possible institutions for the general diffusion of knowledge. In proportion as the structure of a government gives force to public opinion, it is essential that public opinion should be enlightened."

Benjamin Franklin (Founding Father): "An investment in knowledge pays the best interest."

How This Connects to Our Plan

  • Latin is essential — Every founder studied Latin for years. Jefferson, Adams, Washington, Hamilton, Madison, and Jay all read Latin classics in the original language.
  • Logic and rhetoric matter — The founders were trained in formal logic and persuasive speech. Our Logic and Rhetoric stages mirror their educational journey.
  • Bible is foundational — The founders cited Scripture constantly. Education was inseparable from moral and religious formation.
  • Civics and government — Founders studied political philosophy (Locke, Montesquieu, Blackstone) to design a government. Our plan teaches this explicitly.
  • Classics build character — Reading great books wasn't just intellectual exercise—it was moral formation. The founders believed character preceded liberty.

Unit Study / Interest-Led Learning (Alternative)

Organizes learning around a central theme or interest, combining multiple subjects into one project (e.g., a unit on ancient Egypt covering history, science, math, reading, and art).

Pros

  • Highly engaging and personalized
  • Connects subjects naturally
  • Flexibility to follow the child's interests
  • Great for hands-on, project-based learners

Cons

  • May leave gaps in foundational skills
  • Requires significant planning effort
  • Harder to track academic progress
  • Less structured for math/science sequencing

Charlotte Mason (Alternative)

Based on the philosophy of English educator Charlotte Mason (1842–1923), emphasizing "living books," nature study, short lessons, and education as an "atmosphere, discipline, and life."

Pros

  • Rich literature and nature-based learning
  • Short lessons suit shorter attention spans
  • Emphasizes imagination and creativity
  • Lessens pressure of "school-at-home"

Cons

  • Limited math/science structure
  • Requires expensive "living books"
  • May not provide enough rigor for advanced learners
  • Less emphasis on systematic grammar/logic

Our Hybrid Approach

  • Structured enough for a non-teacher parent to follow
  • Deep in math and science with daily hands-on time
  • Rich in literacy and communication skills

3. The Trivium: Three Pillars of Classical Learning

The Trivium (Latin for "the three ways") is the foundation of classical education. Each stage corresponds to a child's natural learning tendencies and builds progressively:

Grammar

Grammar Stage

Ages 5–11 (K–6)

The "what" stage — children naturally absorb facts, rules, vocabulary, and patterns through repetition, songs, memorization, and hands-on exploration.

Logic

Logic Stage

Ages 11–14 (7–9)

The "why" stage — children begin to question, analyze, and reason. They want to understand how things work and why rules exist.

Rhetoric

Rhetoric Stage

Ages 14–18 (10–12)

The "how" stage — teenagers develop the ability to synthesize, persuade, create, and express their own voice and ideas clearly.

Jeffersonian Connection: Jefferson believed the ages 8–16 represent the "prime window" for memory and language acquisition. The Grammar and Logic stages align perfectly with this window — this is when Latin, math, and facts should be memorized heavily. By the Rhetoric stage, the child has the foundation to reason and communicate effectively.

4. Grammar Stage (Ages 5–11, Grades K–6)

Grammar Stage: Building the Foundation

"Memorize the rules before you question them." — Dorothy Sayers

In the Grammar Stage, children absorb information like sponges. The focus is on building a rich foundation of facts, vocabulary, rules, and patterns through repetition, songs, games, memorization, and hands-on exploration. This is the perfect time to introduce Latin (per Jefferson's language-acquisition window) and to develop strong reading and math habits.

Mathematics JEFFERSONIAN EMBED

Goal: Mastery of arithmetic, fractions, geometry basics, and measurement through concrete and visual methods.

  • Grades K–2: Counting, addition/subtraction facts, shapes, measurement, money, simple patterns
  • Grades 3–4: Multiplication/division facts, long division, fractions, decimals, area/perimeter, word problems
  • Grades 5–6: Integers, ratios, percentages, volume, coordinate planes, introductory algebra (solving equations)
  • Jeffersonian Addition: Weekly geometry puzzles using manipulatives (pattern blocks, geoboards); navigation exercises (mapping, compass work, scale drawings)

Language Arts

Goal: Reading fluency, vocabulary expansion, grammar rules, and foundational writing skills.

  • Grades K–2: Phonics, sight words, reading aloud daily, simple sentences, copywork (copying good writing), drawing + describing pictures
  • Grades 3–4: Grammar rules (parts of speech, sentences), paragraph writing, book reports, poetry memorization, vocabulary lists, spelling bees
  • Grades 5–6: Essay structure (introduction, body, conclusion), research projects, grammar analysis, creative writing, public speaking (short presentations)
  • Reading: Read aloud together daily; begin chapter books, biographies, mythology (Greek, Norse, Roman)

Science JEFFERSONIAN EMBED

Goal: Curiosity-driven exploration of the natural world through observation, hands-on experiments, and nature journals.

  • Grades K–2: Plants, animals, weather, five-sense observation, nature walks, simple experiments (sink/float, magnets), nature journaling
  • Grades 3–4: Life cycles, ecosystems, simple machines, weather patterns, basic chemistry (mixing substances), science fair projects
  • Grades 5–6: Earth science (rocks, minerals, plate tectonics), basic physics (forces, motion, energy), human body systems, chemistry intro (elements, periodic table)
  • Jeffersonian Addition: Monthly "practical science" projects — build a simple bridge, measure rainfall, track moon phases, create a weather log

History & Geography

Goal: Build a timeline of world history and geographical awareness through stories, maps, and memorization.

  • Grades K–2: Family history, local community helpers, simple maps, continents/oceans, holidays from around the world, fairy tales and folklore
  • Grades 3–4: Ancient civilizations (Egypt, Greece, Rome), medieval Europe, simple timelines, map skills (latitude/longitude basics)
  • Grades 5–6: American history (colonies through Civil War), U.S. geography, primary source documents, historical figures biographies

Latin / Foreign Language

Goal: Begin Latin in Grade 1 to capitalize on the memory peak. Introduce a second modern language in Grade 4.

  • Grades 1–3: Latin alphabet, basic vocabulary, simple sentences, Roman mythology, Latin through songs and games
  • Grades 4–6: Latin grammar (declensions, conjugations), reading simplified Latin texts, Spanish begins (conversational, songs, basic grammar)
  • Jeffersonian Connection: "Latin is the key that unlocks the door to the languages of science and the classics."

Arts & Creative Expression

  • Drawing, painting, coloring — daily free art time
  • Classical art appreciation (looking at paintings, discussing colors and shapes)
  • Music: singing, rhythm games, basic instrument introduction (keyboard, ukulele, or recorder)
  • Crafts: make things related to history/science topics (build Roman columns, paint landscapes)

Physical Education

  • Daily outdoor play (minimum 30 minutes)
  • Basic sports: tag, relay races, ball games
  • Yoga or simple stretching routines
  • Swimming lessons (if available)
  • Nature hikes and exploration walks

5. Logic Stage (Ages 11–14, Grades 7–9)

Logic Stage: Questioning and Reasoning

"The child who can ask 'why?' is ready to learn."

In the Logic Stage, children naturally begin to question everything. They want to understand how things work, debate ideas, and analyze arguments. This is the perfect time to introduce formal logic, structured argumentation, deeper scientific inquiry, and more rigorous mathematics. The Jeffersonian emphasis on geometry and navigation becomes central.

Mathematics JEFFERSONIAN EMBED

Goal: Develop logical reasoning through algebra, formal geometry, and problem-solving.

  • Grade 7: Algebra 1 (variables, equations, inequalities, functions)
  • Grade 8: Geometry (proofs, triangles, circles, area/volume, coordinate geometry)
  • Grade 9: Algebra 2 (quadratic equations, polynomials, logarithms) + Trigonometry basics
  • Jeffersonian Addition: Weekly geometry proofs and constructions with compass/straightedge; navigation projects (triangulation, map reading, simple surveying); real-world problem sets (budgeting, measurements)

Language Arts

Goal: Develop analytical reading, structured argumentation, and persuasive writing.

  • Reading: Full novels (classic literature), nonfiction, historical documents, Shakespeare (beginning in Grade 8)
  • Grammar: Advanced grammar (sentences, clauses, punctuation), syntax analysis
  • Writing: Argumentative essays, analytical essays, book reports with thesis statements, research papers with citations
  • Debate: Simple debates on current events, literary analysis, logical fallacies identification
  • Vocabulary: Latin-root words, SAT-prep vocabulary lists, word games

Science JEFFERSONIAN EMBED

Goal: Deep scientific understanding through the scientific method, lab work, and theoretical study.

  • Grade 7: Biology (cells, genetics, ecology, biblical creation) with dissections and microscope work
  • Grade 8: Chemistry (atomic structure, periodic table, chemical reactions, balancing equations) with lab experiments
  • Grade 9: Physics (motion, forces, energy, waves, electricity) with experiments and mathematical applications
  • Jeffersonian Addition: Monthly "practical science" projects — build a simple circuit, create a chemical volcano, study local flora/fauna, measure and analyze data, write lab reports
  • Hands-on emphasis: Every science unit includes at least 2 hands-on experiments or field studies

History & Geography

Goal: Understand cause-and-effect in history, analyze primary sources, and develop geographical literacy.

  • Grade 7: World history (Renaissance, Reformation, Age of Exploration)
  • Grade 8: U.S. history (Constitution, Civil War, Reconstruction, Industrial Revolution)
  • Grade 9: World history (French Revolution, Industrial Revolution, World Wars, Modern Era)
  • Skills: Primary source analysis, cause-and-effect essays, map work, geographic reasoning
  • Jeffersonian Addition: Study of government and civics — Jefferson's own writings, the Declaration of Independence, Federalist Papers

Latin / Foreign Language

Goal: Advanced Latin grammar and reading; second language fluency; optional third language.

  • Grade 7: Latin (Caesar's Gallic War readings, advanced grammar), Spanish (conversational fluency, reading simple texts)
  • Grade 8: Latin (Cicero, Roman history), Spanish (intermediate grammar, literature)
  • Grade 9: Latin (Virgil's Aeneid, advanced composition), Spanish (advanced literature, writing), optional third language (French, German, or Greek)
  • Jeffersonian Connection: "The mind acquires new faculties at certain ages, and loses them at others." — Latin and language study during the memory peak window.

Arts & Creative Expression

  • Art history and analysis (moving from appreciation to critical evaluation)
  • Portfolio development in one or two art forms (drawing, painting, photography, digital art)
  • Music: continued instrument study or music theory, ensemble/group performance
  • Creative writing: poetry, short stories, journaling

Physical Education

  • Daily physical activity (minimum 30 minutes)
  • Team sports (basketball, soccer, volleyball)
  • Individual fitness (running, swimming, cycling)
  • Self-defense or martial arts introduction
  • Nature hikes, camping trips, outdoor adventures

6. Rhetoric Stage (Ages 14–18, Grades 10–12)

Rhetoric Stage: Synthesizing and Expressing

"The ability to think clearly, argue well, and write powerfully."

In the Rhetoric Stage, teenagers develop the ability to think independently, argue persuasively, and create original work. They can now synthesize knowledge from all subjects and express their own voice with clarity and power. This is the time for college prep, advanced studies, leadership, and preparing for civic participation.

Mathematics JEFFERSONIAN EMBED

Goal: Advanced mathematical reasoning, preparation for college-level math, and real-world applications.

  • Grade 10: Geometry (formal proofs, advanced theorems) or Pre-Calculus (trigonometry, complex numbers, conic sections)
  • Grade 11: Calculus (differential and integral) or Statistics and Probability
  • Grade 12: Multivariable Calculus, Linear Algebra, or AP Calculus BC / AP Statistics
  • Jeffersonian Addition: Applied mathematics — engineering projects, financial math (compound interest, investments), data analysis, coding with Python for math visualization

Language Arts

Goal: Advanced literary analysis, persuasive writing, public speaking, and development of a personal voice.

  • Reading: Great Books program (Plato, Aristotle, Augustine, Milton, Dickens, Tolstoy, Twain), Shakespeare complete works, philosophy texts
  • Writing: College-level essays, research papers with annotations, creative nonfiction, journalism, op-eds
  • Oratory: Formal speeches, debate competitions, mock trial, student-led seminars
  • Analysis: Literary criticism, rhetorical analysis, philosophical argumentation
  • Portfolio: Compile a writing portfolio by graduation

Science JEFFERSONIAN EMBED

Goal: College-level science with independent research, lab work, and interdisciplinary connections.

  • Grade 10: Advanced Biology (molecular biology, genetics, biochemistry) or Environmental Science
  • Grade 11: Advanced Chemistry (organic chemistry, thermodynamics) or Physics (electromagnetism, optics)
  • Grade 12: Senior Thesis / Independent Research Project in a chosen scientific area; AP Science exams if desired
  • Jeffersonian Addition: Capstone science project — design and execute an original experiment; publish findings or present at a science fair; connect science to ethics, philosophy, and society
  • Technology Integration: Use technology for data collection, analysis, and visualization; introduce coding (Python, R) for scientific computing

History & Geography

Goal: Advanced historical analysis, political philosophy, and global awareness.

  • Grade 10: American Government and Civics — Constitution, Bill of Rights, current events analysis
  • Grade 11: European History ( Enlightenment, Revolutions, World Wars, Cold War) with primary source analysis
  • Grade 12: Philosophy and Ethics — Plato, Aristotle, Aquinas, Locke, Rousseau, Kant, modern philosophers; debates on contemporary ethical issues
  • Skills: Historical argumentation, thesis-driven essays, geopolitical analysis, model UN or debate tournaments

Latin / Foreign Language

Goal: Advanced language proficiency, translation, and cultural understanding.

  • Grade 10: Advanced Latin (Cicero, Tacitus, Seneca), Spanish (Baccalaureate level or equivalent)
  • Grade 11: Latin (Catullus, Ovid, advanced composition), second language (intermediate/advanced literature and writing)
  • Grade 12: Senior seminar in Classical Civilization or Comparative Literature; AP Latin or AP Spanish if desired
  • Jeffersonian Connection: "The mind acquires new faculties at certain ages, and loses them at others." — By this stage, the child should be reading classical texts in their original language.

Arts & Creative Expression

  • Advanced art portfolio for college applications or personal development
  • Music: ensemble performance, composition, music theory, or music history
  • Film studies, drama, or creative writing workshops
  • Art history seminar (moving from study to creation/interpretation)

Physical Education

  • Daily physical activity (minimum 30 minutes)
  • Team sports, intramural leagues
  • Individual fitness goals (running, swimming, weight training)
  • Outdoor adventure (hiking, rock climbing, kayaking)
  • Lifelong fitness habits and health education
